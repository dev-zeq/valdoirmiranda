01 Female hormones and the cycle: what's under your control

Not yet completed

Throughout the menstrual cycle, estrogen and progesterone levels rise and fall in a predictable pattern — and this variation is linked to real, though moderate, differences in appetite, metabolism, and perceived energy throughout the month.

During the menstrual phase, when estrogen and progesterone are at their lowest levels, it's common to feel more tired or sluggish. In the second half of the cycle, hunger tends to increase naturally, following the shift in the ratio between the two hormones.

Variability between women — and between cycles in the same woman — is large. Because of this, the strongest evidence today doesn't point to rigid rules by cycle phase, but to a stable foundation across the whole month: varied nutrition, adequate micronutrients, and glycemic stability, adjusting intensity as the body calls for it.

💡 Key Concept — Hormones respond to patterns, not to a single day

A day of more hunger or more tiredness in the cycle is not cause for alarm — it's the consistency of eating and sleep across the entire month that sustains hormonal balance, not an isolated adjustment on one specific day.

02 Sleep and the female hormonal axis

Not yet completed

Sleep and female hormones go hand in hand. Estrogen, when elevated in the first half of the cycle, tends to favor deeper sleep; progesterone also has a calming effect, helping you relax and fall asleep faster.

In the days before menstruation — when both hormones drop sharply — sleep commonly becomes lighter and more fragmented, with more difficulty falling asleep. This pattern usually resolves on its own once the cycle restarts.

An important caveat is worth noting: more recent research shows that cycle symptoms (cramping, sensitivity, anxiety) appear to influence sleep just as much as hormone levels on their own — meaning that treating the symptom can help just as much as understanding the hormone behind it.

🎯 Suggested Practice — Sleep protocol for premenstrual week

Darken the bedroom completely, set a fixed bedtime, and eliminate screens 60 minutes before sleep — with extra attention in the week before your period, when sleep naturally becomes more fragile.

Is it normal to sleep worse in the days before menstruation?+
Yes. The sharp drop in estrogen and progesterone in the days leading up to your period is associated with lighter sleep and more nighttime awakenings. This tends to improve naturally once menstruation begins and hormones reorganize.

03 Movement, strength, and bone vitality

Not yet completed

Strength training is one of the best-documented habits for sustaining female vitality throughout life — and it takes on an even more important role starting in perimenopause, when the drop in estrogen accelerates the natural loss of bone density.

Randomized clinical trials show modest but real gains in bone density in the lumbar spine and femoral neck with regular strength training in postmenopausal women — somewhere between 1% and 2%, with higher-intensity programs tending to produce slightly better results than lower-intensity ones.

Strength training doesn't replace medical follow-up for osteoporosis, but it is one of the few lifestyle interventions with consistent evidence of bone protection — and, unlike supplements, its side effect is more strength and more energy in daily life.

  • Walking or running as the only activity
  • Little or no loading stimulus on the bones
  • Limited bone protection on its own
  • 2 to 3 strength sessions per week
  • Gradual load progression over the months
  • Measurable bone density gains in studies with postmenopausal women

04 Nutrition for female energy

Not yet completed

Iron is one of the nutrients most associated with fatigue in women: it's estimated that around 30% of women between 15 and 49 years old have iron deficiency or anemia, and that proportion tends to rise during the menopause transition.

Vitamin B12 joins iron as another common, under-investigated cause of tiredness — the symptoms of the two overlap, which makes it easy to confuse one with the other without a simple blood test.

Calcium and vitamin D gain extra importance from menopause onward, when bone loss accelerates. Beans, lean meats, and dark leafy greens (iron), eggs and fish (B12), and dairy or leafy greens (calcium) form the dietary base most consistent with what research recommends — without relying on supplementation unless a test confirms the need.

💡 Key Concept — Persistent tiredness is worth investigating, not just compensating for

Fatigue that doesn't improve with adequate sleep and nutrition may have a treatable nutritional cause (iron, B12) — it's worth a conversation with a health professional and a blood test before assuming it's just stress or a hectic routine.

05 Chronic stress and the female body

Not yet completed

Cortisol and the female hormonal axis have a direct, well-documented relationship: high, sustained stress can suppress the release of GnRH, altering cycle regularity and intensifying PMS symptoms.

This helps explain why periods of prolonged overload — at work, in sleep, in personal life — tend to come with irregular cycles, more tiredness, and more premenstrual symptoms, even when diet and training remain adequate.

Some studies associate chronic stress sustained over many years with more intense menopause symptoms — but it's worth noting: genetics remains the strongest single factor in determining the age of menopause, not stress levels.

🎯 Suggested Practice — Daily decompression break

Set aside 15 minutes a day, at the same time, for a screen-free activity with no productivity pressure: walking, conscious breathing, sitting in silence. Sustain it for three weeks before assessing the effect on your cycle and energy.
