Nutrition and Energy

What to eat to keep your mood, focus, and energy steady throughout the day — and what quietly drains your energy without you noticing the cause.

📖 ~17 min read 🗂️ 6 sections 🔒 Content exclusive to students

01 What really drains your energy

Not yet completed

Most people look for energy in stimulants — coffee, sugar, energy drinks — without realizing that the cause of fatigue is often a decision made hours earlier: an unbalanced meal, insufficient hydration, or a poorly used night's sleep.

There are three silent energy thieves that show up in most of the cases I've observed over the years: glycemic spikes, mild dehydration (which already reduces perceived energy before thirst even appears), and overly large meals, which divert blood and energy toward digestion.

Identifying which of these three is your main personal thief already solves a good part of the problem — and that's discovered by observing, not guessing.

💡 Key Concept — Energy is not the same thing as stimulation

Caffeine and sugar stimulate the nervous system, creating a passing sense of alertness. Real energy comes from a well-fed, hydrated, and rested metabolism — it's more stable, lasts longer, and doesn't charge the bill later in the form of a sharp crash.

🎯 Suggested Practice — Energy diary

For three days, write down your energy level from 1 to 5 every two or three hours, along with what you ate and drank beforehand. Patterns emerge quickly — usually by the second day.

02 Blood sugar: the engine behind fatigue

Not yet completed

When we eat an isolated refined carbohydrate — white bread, a sweet, a sugary juice — the body absorbs the sugar quickly, generating a spike in blood glucose. In response, the body releases insulin to bring levels back to normal — and this process often 'overshoots,' producing a drop below the starting level. That drop is felt as fatigue, irritability, or sudden hunger, usually one to two hours after eating.

The good news is that this cycle is avoidable in practice, without eliminating carbohydrates from your life: just never eat them completely on their own. Fiber, protein, and fat, when present in the same meal, slow down sugar absorption and soften both the spike and the crash.

This explains why a piece of bread on its own tires you out faster than the same piece of bread eaten with egg, avocado, or a portion of protein.

Why do I crave something sweet almost every afternoon?+
This pattern almost always points to a glycemic crash after lunch, especially if the meal was low in protein and fiber. The body, sensing energy levels dropping, asks for a quick fix — sugar. Adjusting lunch to include more protein and vegetables usually reduces this specific craving quite a bit.

03 Breakfast: myth, habit, or necessity?

Not yet completed

For generations, breakfast has been presented as 'the most important meal of the day' — a claim repeated so often it's rarely questioned. The truth, observed over decades of study, is more individual: some people perform better eating early, while others feel lighter and more focused delaying their first meal by a few hours.

What matters isn't the time itself, but the quality of the first meal, whether it's at 7 a.m. or at noon. A breakfast rich in simple sugar (white bread, processed juice, sugary cereal) can generate more fatigue than benefit, reversing the logic that 'eating early gives you energy.'

It's worth trying both approaches for a few weeks each and comparing, carefully, how your body responds — rather than following a generic rule that may not suit your metabolism.

  • Ideal for those who feel real hunger on waking
  • Priority: protein + good fat, little sugar
  • Avoids a glycemic spike right at the start of the day
  • Ideal for those who don't feel hungry in the morning
  • Coffee or tea without sugar can fill the gap
  • Many report more mental clarity in the first half of the day
✒️ Author's Observation

I've seen people force themselves to eat early for decades, with discomfort, simply because they'd heard it was 'mandatory.' The body speaks — and it's worth listening to it rather than following a generic rule.

04 Foods that sustain focus throughout the day

Not yet completed

The brain consumes a disproportionate share of the body's energy and is particularly sensitive to swings in glucose. Foods that release energy gradually — low glycemic-index carbohydrates, lean proteins, good fats like olive oil and nuts — tend to sustain concentration longer than pure sugar spikes.

Eggs, nuts, avocado, fatty fish, and legumes come up often among the foods most associated with sustained focus, likely because they combine good fat, protein, and micronutrients in a single source.

It's also worth noting the role of portion size: very large meals divert blood flow toward digestion, temporarily reducing the energy available for other functions, including mental concentration. Moderate, more frequent meals tend to sustain focus more consistently than two huge meals a day.

🎯 Suggested Practice — One-week swap

Pick a snack or meal in your day that's currently based on simple sugar (cookies, soda, candy) and swap it for an option with protein and good fat (nuts, a boiled egg, plain yogurt). Notice the difference in energy over the following two or three hours.

05 Hydration, minerals, and invisible energy

Not yet completed

Mild dehydration — often just 1 to 2% of body weight in water — is already enough to reduce perceived energy, focus, and even mood, usually before the sensation of thirst appears clearly. It's one of the most underestimated energy thieves, precisely because it's silent.

Alongside water, electrolytes play a central role: on hot days, with heavy sweating or physical activity, water alone may not be enough to replace what was lost, and the feeling of fatigue persists even when drinking plenty of fluids.

A simple practice: start the day with a glass of water before coffee, keep a bottle visible throughout the day (simply seeing it already increases intake), and include natural mineral sources like fruit, dark leafy greens, and nuts in your daily diet.

💡 Key Concept — Thirst is a late alarm

By the time you feel thirsty, your body has already been mildly dehydrated for a while. Keeping hydration steady throughout the day, rather than only responding to thirst, is the difference between stable energy and bouts of unexplained fatigue.

06 Quick food guide

Not yet completed

There's no miracle food — and it's worth being suspicious of anyone who promises one. What exists is a diet rich in real food, day after day. Here are some of the ones I use and recommend most in practice, with what I've learned about each over the years.

🫒

Olive oil

Monounsaturated fats, omega-9, and vitamins E, A, and K. Mediterranean cultures call it "liquid gold" — always choose extra-virgin, in a dark bottle.

🥑

Avocado

Good fat, fiber, and potassium. Helps keep cholesterol balanced and sustains satiety longer — worth including regularly.

🌱

Chia seeds

Omega-3, antioxidants, calcium, and fiber in a cheap and natural supplement. One tablespoon a day, hydrated, already makes a difference.

🧅

Onion

Vitamins A, C, and E, plus sulfur, selenium, and anthocyanin — an underrated natural antioxidant. Always prefer it freshly cut, at peak freshness.

🌾

Quinoa

Phosphorus, magnesium, and calcium for bone health. Gluten-free and one of the few plant sources with all nine essential amino acids — rare among grains.

🌰

Brazil nuts

The most concentrated source of selenium there is. Three nuts a day already cover a good part of the daily need for this mineral.

🍯

Bee pollen

Proteins, B-complex vitamins, minerals, and antioxidants in a single grain. Traditionally used as a natural ally during low-energy phases.

🥬

Dark leafy greens

Kale, arugula, and spinach are generous sources of antioxidants. Best eaten raw, in salads, without prolonged cooking.

🥕

Tubers and roots

Arracacha and radish are tubers; sweet potato, carrot, and beet are roots. All are energy sources — worth rotating through them over the week.

🍚

Brown rice

Keeps the bran and germ that white rice loses in polishing, which is why it holds more fiber, magnesium, manganese, and B-complex vitamins, plus produces a more stable blood sugar response after the meal. Paired with beans or another legume over the course of the day — it doesn't need to be on the same plate — it helps complete the essential amino acid profile.

🍚 Practical Tip — Fluffy brown rice

The secret isn't the cooking time, it's the order: put the raw rice on the heat in cold water (double the volume of the rice), without salt, until it's almost dry — salt at the start makes the grain harder. Sauté onion and garlic only afterward, and season last. In about 25 minutes it comes out perfectly fluffy.

🎯 Complementary Habit — Sleep well

Energy-restoring sleep begins before you lie down: a dark room, no electronic devices nearby, and the habit of exercising — preferably right after waking — make a real difference in the quality of rest.
