Body, Mind and Habits

The connection between diet, daily habits and mental health — and how small, repeated choices shape an entire life over time.

📖 ~13 min read 🗂️ 5 sections 🔒 Content exclusive to students

01 The gut-brain connection

Not yet completed

The gut is often called the 'second brain' for a concrete reason: it houses a vast network of its own neurons and communicates constantly with the brain via the vagus nerve and substances produced by the gut microbiota itself.

Much of the body's serotonin — a substance associated with well-being and mood regulation — is produced in the gut, not the brain. This helps explain why changes in diet and gut health are often reflected in noticeable shifts in mood and mental clarity.

Feeding the microbiota well — with varied fiber, fermented foods, and fewer ultra-processed foods — isn't just a digestive matter. It is also, indirectly, a way of tending to emotional balance.

💡 Key Concept — A diverse microbiota, a steadier mind

The greater the diversity of fiber and vegetables in the diet, the greater the diversity of beneficial bacteria in the gut tends to be — and this diversity is associated with better mood regulation and stress response.

02 Routine as the architecture of the mind

Not yet completed

The human mind spends significant energy making decisions — including small, repetitive decisions, such as what to wear or what to eat. Well-built routines eliminate much of this micro-decision-making, freeing up mental energy for what really matters during the day.

This phenomenon, sometimes called decision fatigue, explains why people with well-defined morning routines often report more clarity and less anxiety throughout the day — not because they have fewer problems, but because they spend less mental energy on trivial decisions.

Building a routine doesn't mean absolute rigidity. It means setting anchors — wake-up time, the first action of the day, bedtime — and leaving the rest of the day freer to adapt to whatever comes up.

🎯 Suggested Practice — Three anchors to start with

Choose just three fixed anchors for this week: wake-up time, one specific action within the first 10 minutes of the day (water, sunlight, stretching), and bedtime. Don't add anything else until these three are consistent.

Doesn't routine turn into monotony?+
The important distinction is between anchors (a few fixed points that structure the day) and total rigidity (controlling every minute). Effective routines leave generous room for spontaneity within a minimal structure — it is that minimal structure that sustains the freedom of the rest of the day, not the other way around.

03 Diet and mood

Not yet completed

Blood sugar swings don't just affect physical energy — they directly affect mood. Sharp drops in glucose can trigger irritability, sudden anxiety, and difficulty concentrating, symptoms that are often mistakenly attributed to purely emotional causes.

Ultra-processed foods, high in sugar and low in fiber, tend to produce this swing pattern more often. Whole foods, rich in fiber, protein, and healthy fat, sustain a more stable mood throughout the day precisely because they avoid these spikes and crashes.

This doesn't mean diet alone resolves deeper emotional issues — it means diet is one of the physical foundations on which emotional balance rests, and it's worth tending to that foundation first.

💡 Key Concept — Irritability can also be hunger in disguise

The popular term hangry describes a real phenomenon: drops in blood glucose directly affect emotional regulation, not just physical energy.

04 Small habits, big results

Not yet completed

The most underrated effect in decades of observing health is not that of big, one-off changes, but that of small habits repeated consistently — what I like to call the compound effect of habits. One extra glass of water a day, five minutes of walking, one extra serving of vegetables — on their own, almost irrelevant. Over the years, transformative.

The most common trap is seeking total, immediate transformation, giving up at the first obstacle, and concluding that 'it doesn't work'. In practice, what works sustainably is always smaller and slower than initially imagined — and precisely because of that, easier to maintain.

If a habit seems too easy to make a difference, it's probably the right size to start with.

🎯 Suggested Practice — The minimum viable habit

Choose a change so small it seems almost insignificant — drinking a glass of water upon waking, for example — and sustain it for 30 days before adding anything else.

✒️ Author's Note

If I could sum up decades of study in one sentence, it would be this: the body and mind respond more to repetition than to intensity. Prefer the small, sustainable habit over the big effort that lasts a week.

05 Digital disconnection and mental clarity

Not yet completed

The constant consumption of digital stimuli — notifications, endless scrolling, multiple screens at once — keeps the nervous system in a low, continuous state of alertness, making it harder to access deeper states of focus and mental rest, including sleep itself.

Regular periods of disconnection — even short ones, 30 to 60 minutes without a screen — allow the mind to process information in a more organized way, something that rarely happens while actively consuming content.

It's not about eliminating technology, but about creating conscious windows of absence from it, especially in the first and last hours of the day — moments when the mind is most sensitive to the quality of the stimuli it receives.

  • Phone as the first and last thing of the day
  • Constant notifications throughout the day
  • Feeling of scatteredness and difficulty with deep focus
  • First hour of the day without a screen
  • Focus blocks with notifications turned off
  • Last hour before sleep without a screen
