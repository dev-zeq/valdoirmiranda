Food Combining Guide

How to organize what you eat — not just what, but in what order and in what company — to digest better, absorb more, and have more energy left at the end of the day.

📖 ~14 min read 🗂️ 5 sections 🔒 Content exclusive to students

01 Why combining matters

Not yet completed

We spent decades being taught to think of food as a list of isolated items: protein here, carbohydrate there, a piece of fruit for dessert. But the body doesn't digest isolated items — it digests entire meals, and the way foods meet in the stomach determines how much effort the digestive system will spend and how much energy will be left over for the rest of the body.

Each type of food calls for a different chemical environment and a different digestion time. When these requirements clash within the same plate, the result is that feeling of heaviness, drowsiness after lunch, and a digestion that drags on for hours. It isn't a lack of willpower — it's basic physiology being ignored.

This module doesn't propose rigid rules or lists of prohibitions. It proposes a way of thinking: understanding the logic behind combining so that you can start building smarter plates yourself, at your own pace and with the foods that are already part of your routine.

💡 Key Concept — Sequential digestion

The stomach adjusts its acidity and its rhythm according to the predominant type of food. When you mix groups with very different requirements in large quantities, the body has to negotiate — and that negotiation consumes time and energy.

🎯 Suggested Practice — A week of observation

Before changing anything, just observe. Note how you feel 90 minutes after each meal: light, normal, or heavy. This simple record already reveals your personal combining patterns before any adjustment.

✒️ Author's Note

There's no universal chart that works perfectly for every body. What I teach here is a method of observation — decades of study have shown me that the individual response always has the final word.

02 Proteins and carbohydrates: the competing duo

Not yet completed

The most discussed — and most misunderstood — combination is that of concentrated proteins with dense carbohydrates on the same plate, in the same volume. It isn't that this combination is forbidden; it's that, when both appear in large quantity, digesting one can compete for the space and time of the other.

A practical way to think about it: instead of asking "what can I eat together," ask "which of these two groups will be the star of this meal." When one dominates the plate and the other appears in a moderate amount, digestion tends to flow with less friction.

Vegetables and greens, on the other hand, practically never compete with anything — they're the neutral element that eases digestion of any combination, working almost as a mediator between groups.

What about rice and beans, Brazil's most traditional dish?+
Rice and beans isn't a problem in itself — it's a combination tested across generations and nutritionally complementary in amino acids. The issue isn't eliminating it, but watching the volume: plates with moderate portions of both, plus vegetables, tend to be better tolerated than plates where both appear in maximum quantity, with no vegetable at all to balance them.
  • Large portion of rice
  • Large portion of meat
  • Beans in a generous amount
  • Little or no raw vegetables

Three dense groups competing for digestive attention at the same time — common, but not always the lightest option.

  • Half the plate in vegetables and greens
  • One star: protein OR carbohydrate in the larger portion
  • The second group in a moderate portion
  • Good fat (olive oil, avocado) for satiety

Same food, different proportion — less competition, more perceived lightness.

03 Fruit: alone or with company?

Not yet completed

Fruit moves quickly through the stomach when eaten on its own — in many cases, within minutes. The problem shows up when it's eaten *after* a heavy meal, as dessert: it gets held up behind foods that digest more slowly, and that waiting time can favor fermentation and discomfort for some people.

An old and simple practice — eating fruit about 20 to 30 minutes before a meal, or at a separate time, as a snack — usually resolves much of this complaint. It isn't an absolute rule, but a timing adjustment that many people report feeling a real difference from.

An interesting exception: fatty fruits like avocado and coconut behave differently from fruits rich in simple sugar, and tolerate the company of other foods well, since their digestion is closer to that of a fat than to that of a sugar.

💡 Key Concept — Gastric transit of fruit

Fruits rich in water and natural sugar pass quickly through the stomach when eaten alone. Combined with slow-digesting foods, this quick transit gets blocked — not by the fruit, but by the slower food accompanying it.

🎯 Suggested Practice — The isolated-fruit test

For a week, try eating fruit at a time separate from your main meals — in the morning, mid-afternoon, or as part of a stand-alone snack. Compare how you feel to eating it as dessert.

04 Fiber, good fats, and the rhythm of digestion

Not yet completed

Fiber is the great regulator of the digestive process: it slows the absorption of sugars, adds bulk and satiety, and feeds the community of bacteria living in the gut. A plate with little fiber and a lot of refined carbohydrate tends to pass through the intestine too quickly, generating spikes of energy followed by sharp crashes.

Good fats — olive oil, nuts, avocado, seeds — play a similar role: they slow gastric emptying in a healthy way, increase the feeling of satiety, and help absorb the fat-soluble vitamins present in the vegetables on the same plate.

The ideal combination, in practice, almost always involves all three elements working together: a star (protein or carbohydrate), fiber in abundance (vegetables and greens), and a moderate source of good fat. This trio is what sustains stable energy for longer.

Why do I feel sleepy after lunch even when I eat 'right'?+
Post-lunch drowsiness has several possible causes: the total volume of the meal, the proportion of refined carbohydrates, insufficient hydration, and even the quality of the previous night's sleep. Food combining is one piece of the puzzle — reducing total volume and prioritizing fiber tends to bring the most noticeable effect.
✒️ Author's Note

I've learned, over the years, that fiber and good fat do more for the feeling of stable energy than any single combining rule. If I could give just one piece of advice in this module, it would be: never build a plate without greens or vegetables on it.

05 Building the plate: a practical method

Not yet completed

After decades of testing variations, I arrived at a simple structure that sums up practically everything in this module in a single visual gesture: mentally divide the plate into three parts. Half for a variety of vegetables and greens. A quarter for the star of the meal — protein or carbohydrate, whichever makes more sense at that moment. The last quarter for the supporting act, in a more modest portion, plus a source of good fat.

This method works because it solves, all at once, the three points discussed so far: it reduces competition between dense groups, ensures enough fiber to regulate the process, and includes good fat for satiety and nutrient absorption.

No method replaces observing your own body. Use this structure as a starting point, not as dogma — adjust it according to your routine, your physical activity, and how you feel in the hours that follow.

🎯 Suggested Practice — Apply the method today

At your next main meal, build the plate following the half/quarter/quarter division before you serve yourself. Don't change the foods — just the proportions — and notice the difference on the very first try.
